  "0": "1 Introduction\nDriven by the growing severe energy exhaustion and environmental pollution, the exploitation of next-generation renewable energy resources to replace traditional fossil fuel is becoming more urgent.[1] Electrochemical water splitting has been confirmed to be an effective technique for renewable energy conversion and storage.[2] For example, due to its advantages of high efficiency and zero carbon emission, electrochemical water splitting shows promising potential on the scalable production of high-purity hydrogen.[3] Usually, the efficiency of electrochemical water splitting mainly depends on its two half-cell reactions, including oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER).[4] In particular, OER has been identified as the rate-limiting step of electrochemical water splitting due to the sluggish reaction kinetics attributed to its complex four-electron transfer reaction, which will lead to large overpotential and low cycle efficiency.[5] Therefore, to overcome the dynamic obstacles, numerous researches have been devoted into the development of high-performance electrocatalysts for OER.\nVarious kinds of electrocatalysts have been developed to reduce the overpotential and increase the reaction rate of OER. Although noble metal-based oxides, such as IrO2 and RuO2 which are considered as the state-of-art electrocatalysts for OER, can deliver excellent catalytic activity toward OER, the high cost and low abundance of these materials seriously limit their large-scale commercial applications.[6] In addition, these noble metal-based electrocatalysts usually suffer from poor durability.[7] Hence, it is of great importance to develop high-efficient, low-cost, and robust electrocatalysts for OER. Recently, transition metal oxides have emerged to be a series of potential electrocatalysts for OER, owing to their abundant reserves and promising activity.[8] Among various kinds of transition metal oxides, Co-based oxides, for example, Co3O4 and CoO, are highly appealing candidates for OER due to their high catalytic activity and good long-term stability.[9] However, the further improvement of Co-based oxides in catalytic performance is hindered by their poor intrinsic activity.\nRational design of chemical composition and geometric structure of electrocatalysts has been identified as effective strategies to improve their catalytic performance. Numerous researches have confirmed that the OER activity of Co-based oxides can be remarkably improved by doping Co-based oxides with other functional elements such as Fe, Ni, and Mn.[10] The synergistic effects between Co and the introduced metals can optimize the electronic structure of the host material, leading to significant enhancement in electrical conductivity and more active sites can be exposed.[11] Except for composition control, structural design also plays a crucial role in the improvement of the catalytic performance for Co-based oxides. Hollow structure, which has been widely used in the structural design of various catalysts, is also attractive in the design of high-performance OER catalysts. The large specific surface area of the hollow structure endows materials with abundant active sites to facilitate the multistep process in OER. Meanwhile, the hollow structure can provide a short proton-coupled electron transfer path for OER, resulting in improving the diffusion efficiency.[12] Although numerous efforts have been devoted to the systematic optimization of the composition or structure of Co-based oxide electrocatalysts, the preparation of Co-based oxides with both desirable composition and structure usually suffers from multistep procedures and unsatisfactory controllability. Therefore, it is still challenging to develop facile synthetic protocols to achieve the precise control on the composition and structure of Co-based oxides catalysts for OER.[13]\nHerein, we reported a facile and efficient synthetic protocol for the preparation of Fe-doped CoO nanotubes (denoted as Fe–CoO NTs) as a high-performance electrocatalyst for OER. This synthetic protocol showed good controllability on the composition and structure of this Fe–CoO NT. Without using templates, hollow tubular structure could be well constructed via a simple wet-chemical reaction and a subsequent calcination process. Avoiding taking any extra procedures, Fe doping was achieved by in situ introducing iron salt during the wet-chemical reaction. Characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), the synthesized sample showed a satisfying hollow tubular structure; meanwhile, Fe species were uniformly dispersed into the framework of CoO NTs without any agglomerates. Contributing to the unique hollow structure and the synergistic effect between Fe and Co, this Fe–CoO NT was demonstrated to be a high-performance electrocatalyst for OER. In the synthetic protocol, a wet-chemical reaction was taken first to prepare Fe/Co-based nanorods as the precursor. Anhydrous ethanol and urea were used as solvent and precipitant, respectively. At first, iron salt, cobalt salt, and urea were completely dissolved in anhydrous ethanol to form a transparent mixed solution. Then, this mixed solution was heated to 80 °C and reacted at this temperature for 8 h. During the high-temperature reaction, urea slowly decomposed to release alkaline substrates such as OH−. These alkaline substrates further reacted with transition metal ions (Co2+ and Fe2+) to generate Fe/Co-based precipitates as the precursor. Meanwhile, the adsorption of OH− ions on the Fe/Co-based precipitates would enhance the polarity of the precursor. Therefore, the electrostatic force and hydrogen bonding induced the self-assembly of the Fe/Co-based precursor, leading to the formation of this special nanorod morphology.[14] After following calcination of the precursor at 400 °C, organic compounds were thermal decomposed, and then the hollow structure was formed. In the meantime, due to the crystallization of the transition metal species, the composition of the precursor was transformed to CoO with Fe species dispersed uniformly into the framework, finally forming the hoped-for Fe–CoO NT.
